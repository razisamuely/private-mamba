Safe Dreamers: Proactive Safety in Multi-Agent Reinforcement
Learning with Model-Based World Models
Anonymous Submission

Abstract
Deploying multi-agent reinforcement learning
(MARL) in safety-critical applications requires
both strict safety guarantees and sample efficiency. Existing constrained policy optimization methods are inherently reactive, requiring agents to experience real-world failures to
learn safety, while model-based approaches often ignore safety constraints entirely. We propose Safe Dreamers, the first framework unifying multi-agent world models with Lagrangian
safety constraints. By learning to predict costs
in a latent space, Safe Dreamers enables agents
to be proactive: they learn to avoid safety
violations in imagination, removing the need
to experience them in reality. Evaluated on
the StarCraft Multi-Agent Challenge (SMAC)
with strict casualty constraints, Safe Dreamers achieves competitive task performance with
25× greater sample efficiency than baselines,
successfully satisfying constraints where reactive methods fail.

1

Introduction

Deploying multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL)
in real-world applications requires satisfying two often
conflicting objectives: maximizing task performance and
strictly adhering to safety constraints. In domains such
as autonomous warehouse logistics, coordinated drone
fleets, and power grid management, agents must learn
complex cooperative behaviors without causing catastrophic failures during the learning process. However,
standard RL relies on trial-and-error, posing unacceptable risks in safety-critical environments where a single
violation can be catastrophic.
Current approaches to Safe Reinforcement Learning (Safe RL)—defined as learning policies that maximize reward while satisfying a set of cost constraints—typically rely on constrained policy optimization methods, such as MACPO [Gu et al., 2021]. While
effective at converging to safe policies eventually, these
model-free methods suffer from severe sample inefficiency, often requiring millions of environment interac-

tions to learn even simple tasks. Conversely, modelbased MARL approaches, such as MAMBA [Egorov and
Shpilman, 2022], have demonstrated remarkable sample
efficiency by learning world models that simulate environment dynamics. Yet, these methods traditionally focus solely on reward maximization, ignoring safety constraints and leaving agents blind to the potential costs
of their actions during planning.
This creates a critical gap: practitioners are forced
to choose between the sample efficiency of model-based
methods and the safety guarantees of constrained policy
optimization. To bridge this gap, we propose that the
key to safe and efficient MARL lies in imagination: if
agents can accurately predict the safety consequences
of their actions within a learned world model, they can
learn to avoid unsafe behaviors without ever executing
them in the real world.
We introduce Safe Dreamers, the first framework that
integrates multi-agent world models with Lagrangian
safety constraints. Our approach extends the world
model to jointly learn the dynamics of both rewards and
costs in a latent space, enabling agents to ”dream” potential futures and evaluate their safety during training.
By optimizing policies within this constrained imagination, Safe Dreamers learns to satisfy safety constraints
proactively rather than reactively.
Our contributions are as follows:
• We propose Safe Dreamers, a novel Model-Based
Safe MARL framework that unifies world model
learning with Lagrangian constraint optimization.
• We introduce a Cost-Aware World Model architecture that predicts safety costs alongside system dynamics, enabling interpretable safety planning in latent space.
• We empirically demonstrate that Safe Dreamers
achieves 25× greater sample efficiency than stateof-the-art model-free baselines (MACPO) on the
StarCraft Multi-Agent Challenge (SMAC).
• We show that our method successfully satisfies strict
safety constraints (e.g., zero friendly fire or casualty
limits) in scenarios where baselines fail to find any
feasible solution.

Related Work

The fundamental limitation of current Safe RL is its reliance on real-world failure. Traditional approaches formulate safety as a Constrained MDP [Altman, 1999] and
enforce constraints via Lagrangian relaxation [Liu et al.,
2020] or trust-region updates [Achiam et al., 2017]. In
the multi-agent domain, methods like MACPO [Gu et
al., 2021] successfully extend these disparate constraints
to cooperative settings. However, theoretical guarantees aside, these methods are inherently reactive: they
require the agent to sample unsafe trajectories in the
environment to estimate the cost gradients. In practice,
this means agents must ”learn by dying”—accumulating
thousands of safety violations to map out the dangerous regions of the state space. For systems like drone
swarms, retroactive learning is unacceptable; reliance on
extensive real-world failures should be replaced by learning from imagined violations, drastically reducing the
cost of safety.
Model-based reinforcement learning (MBRL) offers a
path to reduce real-world interaction by substituting
it with planning in a learned world model [Hafner et
al., 2020; Krinner et al., 2025]. In multi-agent domains, methods like MAMBA [Egorov and Shpilman,
2022] have successfully scaled this ”imagination” to complex coordination tasks, achieving high sample efficiency.
However, these frameworks have traditionally focused
on a single objective: reward maximization. Without
an explicit mechanism to represent and predict risk, the
”imagination” of these agents remains incomplete. They
can plan for victory but cannot plan for safety, leaving them vulnerable to the same catastrophic failures as
standard RL, albeit with fewer samples. The missing
piece is a world model that understands danger as well
as it understands dynamics.
We propose that the solution lies in combining these
paradigms to enable proactive safety. Recent singleagent efforts like SafeDreamer [Huang et al., 2024] have
demonstrated that world models can be augmented to
predict costs, allowing agents to ”feel” the penalty of
a safety violation in imagination without executing it.
While other multi-agent approaches have attempted distinct safety mechanisms such as shielding [Xiao et al.,
2023], they often rely on hand-crafted rules or simplified
transition models. Safe Dreamers generalizes the proactive imagination of SafeDreamer to the multi-agent setting, providing the first fully learned, end-to-end framework where teams of agents learn to avoid danger by
optimizing policies within a safe, imagined simulator.

Methodology

We present Safe Dreamers, a framework for safe multiagent coordination that leverages world models to
strictly enforce safety constraints. In this section, we
first formally define the problem as a Constrained DecPOMDP. We then detail our proactive safety mechanism
via latent imagination and present the full algorithm and
network architecture.

3.1

Preliminaries

We formulate the safe multi-agent coordination problem as a Constrained Decentralized Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (C-Dec-POMDP), defined
by the tuple M = ⟨I, S, A, P, R, C, Ω, O, γ, d⟩. Here,
I = {1, . . . , N } is the set of agents; S is the global
state space; A = ×i∈I Ai is the joint action space; and
O = ×i∈I Oi is the joint observation space. At each
timestep t, each agent i observes a partial observation
oit ∈ Oi and selects an action ait ∈ Ai . The environment transitions to a new state st+1 ∼ P(·|st , at ) based
on the joint action at , yielding a shared global reward
rt = R(st , at ) and a shared safety cost ct = C(st , at ).
The objective is to find a joint policy π that P
maximizes
∞
the expected discounted return JR (π) = Eπ [ t=0 γ t rt ]
subject to a safetyPconstraint on the expected discounted
∞
cost JC (π) = Eπ [ t=0 γ t ct ] ≤ d, where d is a pre-defined
safety threshold.

Once the world model captures the safety dynamics, the
agent uses it to generate rollouts for policy optimization. In this phase, we freeze the world model and train
the actor and critic entirely within the latent imagination. The agent generates imagined trajectories of horizon H = 15 starting from states s0 sampled from the
replay buffer. At each step τ of the imagination, the actor πϕ (aτ |sτ ) selects an action. The world model then
performs a two-step forward pass: first, the recurrent

to prioritize safety in the next imagination phase. If the
agent is safe, β decreases, allowing it to pursue higher
rewards.

3.3

Network Architecture

All networks use 256 hidden dimensions, as illustrated
in Figure 2. The world model consists of: (1) encoder
and decoder (2-layer MLPs), (2) RSSM with 256-dim
deterministic state (GRU) and 1024-dim stochastic state
(32 categorical distributions, 32 classes each), (3) 3-layer
self-attention in the transition model (0.1 dropout), and
(4) reward/cost predictors (2-layer MLPs). The actor is
a 4-layer MLP outputting action logits. The dual critic
uses single-layer self-attention followed by separate heads
for V r and V c . Full architecture specifications are provided in Appendix C.
In the multi-agent setting, each agent has its own
encoder and actor (decentralized execution), while the
world model and critic are centralized. Coordination is
achieved through self-attention in the RSSM transition
(3 layers) and critic (1 layer).

3.4

Execution

During execution, each agent maintains its own copy
of the world model to enable decentralized decisionmaking, consistent with the Centralized Training with
Decentralized Execution (CTDE) paradigm. At each decision step, agents exchange latent states and action logits through a multi-head attention mechanism (8 heads
with positional encoding), implementing all-to-all communication across agents. The attention block is applied
before action selection, aggregating teammate information with computational complexity O(n2 · d) per step,
where n is the number of agents and d is the feature
dimension. Each agent then uses its observation and
the attention-aggregated information to select actions
via the trained actor policy.
While standard self-attention scales quadratically with
the number of agents, this cost is negligible for the agent
counts typical in SMAC (up to n = 27). For massivescale scenarios (e.g., hundreds of agents), this communication block can be seamlessly replaced with more scalable alternatives without altering the core Safe Dreamers
framework. Valid options include Mean Field approximations [Yang et al., 2018] which reduce complexity to
O(1) by averaging neighbor embeddings, or sparse attention mechanisms [Mao et al., 2022] that limit interaction
to the k-nearest neighbors for O(n · k) scaling. In our
evaluated domain, however, the all-to-all attention provides the necessary expressivity to capture complex coordination patterns without hitting computational bottlenecks.

4

Experiments

For all results, we report Safe Dreamers’ performance
at 100k steps for a fair comparison, though further improvements are possible with continued training.
We test the hypothesis that learning safety in latent
imagination is superior to real-world failure learning, addressing three research questions:
• RQ1 (Proactive Safety): Can Safe Dreamers
maintain high performance under strict safety constraints where reactive methods fail?
• RQ2 (Sample Efficiency): Does learning in
imagination quantifiably reduce the real-world samples needed to converge to a safe policy?
• RQ3 (Mechanism): Are the world model’s cost
predictions and the Lagrangian mechanism essential
for this success? ( addressed in Section 4.3).

4.1

Experimental Setup

Domain: StarCraft II (SMAC). We evaluate our
framework on the StarCraft Multi-Agent Challenge
(SMAC) [Samvelyan et al., 2019], a standard benchmark
for complex multi-agent coordination. SMAC requires
precise micro-management and coordination in a highdimensional state space. We select scenarios ranging
from homogeneous units (e.g., 3m, 8m) to heterogeneous
teams (e.g., 1c3s5z, 2s3z ) to test the generalizability of
our method. See Appendix B for game rules, scenario
maps, and visualization details.
Safety Constraint: Casualty Aversion. We define the safety cost as the number of dead allied units per
episode. This metric is particularly challenging because
it is sparse (deaths occur only at specific moments), irreversible, and critical for real-world deployment where as-

Mechanism Analysis (RQ3)

To answer RQ3 (”Are the world model’s cost predictions
and the Lagrangian mechanism essential?”), we conduct
ablation studies on the 8m SMAC scenario. We systematically vary the cost limit d and the loss components to
isolate the contribution of each mechanism. Additionally, we verify the necessity of the safety layer by compar-

ing against an unconstrained MAMBA baseline [Egorov
and Shpilman, 2022]; results in Appendix G show that
the unconstrained agent incurs high costs, confirming the
role of our mechanism.
Cost Limit Sensitivity
Sweeping cost limits across 8m and 1c3s5z shows a clear
trend: tighter limits induce larger learned Lagrangian
penalties, and costs stay below the allowed bound; as
limits loosen, penalties decrease and costs smoothly rise
toward the permitted level. The figures below show only
the actual cost versus cost limit for each map.
Across both maps, the controller adapts continuously
rather than flipping abruptly: penalties decline as constraints relax, costs track the target bound, and mission
success stays strong. This trend indicates safety control
does not trade off performance.

Key Findings
Our ablations highlight three core findings. The Lagrangian mechanism robustly adapts to constraint tightness, consistently enforcing constraints while preserving
75–99% win rates; the learned multiplier β scales as expected. The principal contribution is methodological:
the world model’s transparency lets us decompose predictions (cost, reward, dynamics), inspect logged losses,
and debug components, making it straightforward to improve safety across domains without sacrificing performance. Safe Dreamers generalizes across map topologies and unit compositions, with a consistent constraint
threshold despite different optimal operating points between scenarios, indicating the approach extends beyond
single-scenario tuning.

Discussion

Why Imagination-Based Safety Works.
Safe
Dreamers’ superior performance stems from its ability
to anticipate safety violations during imagination rather
than experiencing them in the real environment. The
world model learns to predict both next states and associated costs in latent space, enabling the policy to
evaluate trajectories for safety before execution. When
the cost predictor identifies a potentially unsafe action
sequence during imagination rollouts, the policy can
proactively avoid it. In contrast, model-free methods
like MACPO-Discrete must experience constraint violations through trial-and-error in the actual environment before learning to avoid them. This fundamental difference—proactive safety through imagination versus reactive learning through experience—explains Safe
Dreamers’ 25x sample efficiency advantage and ability to
satisfy strict constraints where MACPO-Discrete completely fails.
Computational Tradeoff. Safe Dreamers at 100k
steps requires comparable wall-clock time (30-50 hours
depending on scenario) to MACPO at 5M steps, reflecting 50x fewer environment steps but higher per-step computation for world model training. This tradeoff favors
Safe Dreamers when environment interaction is expensive or safety-critical.

5

Conclusion

We presented Safe Dreamers, which extends MAMBA’s
imagination-based planning to predict both rewards and
costs in latent space, enabling agents to learn policies
that anticipate and avoid safety violations.
Our evaluation on StarCraft Multi-Agent Challenge
scenarios demonstrates that Safe Dreamers achieves
competitive task performance while satisfying safety
constraints that model-free baselines violate. Explicit
cost modeling provides sample-efficient safe exploration
through imagination-based planning, addressing the fundamental tradeoff between safety and sample efficiency
in multi-agent reinforcement learning.
Limitations. Our current implementation focuses on
cooperative scenarios with centralized training and decentralized execution. The cost prediction accuracy depends on the world model’s ability to capture safetyrelevant dynamics, which may require careful tuning of
the latent space representation.
Future Work. Promising directions include extending Safe Dreamers to competitive and mixed-motive scenarios, investigating hierarchical safety constraints at
different abstraction levels, and developing theoretical
guarantees for constraint satisfaction in imaginationbased planning. Additionally, while we currently use
imagination only for training, future work could explore runtime imagination for anomaly detection, allowing agents to detect and avoid unforeseen hazards during execution without retraining. Incorporating human
feedback to refine cost functions could also improve applicability to real-world safety-critical domains.

Algorithm 1: Safe Dreamers: Safe Multi-Agent Model-Based RL
The Safe Dreamers algorithm operates in three distinct phases within each update step.
Phase 1: Model Learning. The agent updates its world model using experience sampled from the replay
buffer. It learns to reconstruct observations and predict rewards, costs, and state transitions, minimizing a
comprehensive loss function Lmodel that includes reconstruction, prediction, and KL divergence terms.
Phase 2: Proactive Safety via Imagination. This is the core novelty. The agent imagines trajectories in
the latent space of the learned world model. By rolling out policies in imagination, it estimates values and
costs (Vψr and Vψc ) without real-world interaction. The Lagrangian advantage ALag
is computed to penalize

Phase 3: Adaptive Lagrangian Update. The Lagrangian multiplier β is updated based on the actual
costs incurred during the most recent data collection. This ensures the constraint enforcement strength
adapts to the current safety performance, increasing penalty magnitude if the cost limit d is violated.

SMAC Scenario Overview

The StarCraft Multi-Agent Challenge (SMAC) [Samvelyan et al., 2019] is a benchmark for multi-agent reinforcement
learning based on StarCraft II micromanagement. Each scenario uses a different map, featuring unique team compositions and tactical challenges. Allied agents (green circles) face enemy units (red circles); the goal is to defeat all
enemies while minimizing allied losses. Episodes progress in steps, with agents moving, attacking, or using abilities.
In visualizations (e.g., Figure 4), green lines show allied agents shooting. The images illustrate tactical positioning,
combat, and episode outcomes across varied scenarios.

Network Architecture Details

This appendix provides detailed specifications of the network architectures used in Safe Dreamers. All networks use
256 hidden dimensions unless otherwise specified.
We employ Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs) for the Encoder, Reward/Cost Predictors, and Actor/Critic networks
(Table 1) to maintain computational efficiency given the high-frequency interaction required in SMAC. For the
Dynamics Model, we use a Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) to capture the temporal dependencies inherent in partially
observable multi-agent environments. This recurrent structure allows agents to maintain a memory of past events,
which is crucial for coordinating safe maneuvers over extended horizons.

To ensure a rigorous and fair comparison, we adhered to a strict experimental protocol. Both Safe Dreamers and
the MACPO baseline were tuned using the same compute budget via Optuna. We optimized critical hyperparameters including learning rates, entropy coefficients, and clipping bounds. Crucially, while we report Safe Dreamers
performance at 100k steps, we trained baselines for 5 million steps (50× more) to demonstrate that our method’s

Cost Prediction Loss Function Ablation

During development, we observed that the world model’s cost prediction was not sufficiently accurate. This highlights
a key advantage of our approach: the world model enables direct inspection and debugging of cost predictions,
allowing us to identify and address prediction errors. To improve cost prediction accuracy, we evaluated alternative
loss functions beyond the standard mean squared error (MSE), including Huber loss, Focal loss, and Exponential
loss. While these variants showed improvements in cost prediction accuracy, we retained the standard MSE loss in
our final implementation for simplicity and robustness, maintaining focus on the main contribution. This ablation
demonstrates that the framework is extensible and that the world model provides valuable diagnostic capabilities
for iterative improvement—a key methodological advantage that enables principled debugging and optimization of
individual components.
